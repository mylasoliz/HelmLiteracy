AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini have gone from novelty to genuinely useful in the span of a couple of years. People are using them to draft emails, debug code, summarize documents, brainstorm ideas, and learn new skills.

But there's a real risk of over-trusting them. These systems are trained to produce fluent, confident-sounding text — even when they're wrong. Knowing how to use them well means understanding both their strengths and their failure modes.

## What AI Tools Are Actually Good At

**Writing and editing.** AI is remarkably good at drafting, rewriting, summarizing, and adjusting tone. Give it a rough draft and ask it to make it clearer, shorter, or more professional — the results are usually excellent.

**Explaining concepts.** Need to understand how a mortgage amortization schedule works, or what a put option is, or how photosynthesis happens? AI tools are excellent tutors. They can explain things at whatever level of detail you want, answer follow-up questions, and give examples.

**Brainstorming and ideation.** Generating lists of ideas, angles for an essay, names for a project, or approaches to a problem is a perfect use case. You get 20 options in 10 seconds instead of staring at a blank page.

**Summarizing documents.** Paste in a long PDF, contract, or article and ask for a summary. This can save significant reading time, especially for documents heavy in boilerplate.

**Writing and editing code.** For developers, AI has become a genuine productivity multiplier. It can write boilerplate, explain error messages, suggest fixes, and convert code between languages.

## What AI Tools Get Wrong

**Facts, especially specific ones.** Dates, statistics, names, citations — AI tools can and do make these up. This is called "hallucination," and it's not a bug that's going away soon; it's a fundamental limitation of how these systems work.

**Recent events.** Every AI has a knowledge cutoff date. It doesn't know what happened last month unless you tell it.

**Math.** Counterintuitively, many large language models struggle with multi-step arithmetic. They can set up a problem correctly and then miscalculate it.

**Confidently wrong is dangerous.** The biggest risk isn't that AI gives you a hedged, uncertain answer you know to take with a grain of salt — it's that it gives you a polished, authoritative-sounding wrong answer you trust completely.

## How to Prompt Better

The quality of your output depends heavily on the quality of your input. Here's how to get better results:

### Give context

Instead of: *"Write me a cover letter"*

Try: *"I'm applying for a marketing coordinator role at a sustainable fashion startup. I have 2 years of social media experience and I'm transitioning from retail. Write a 3-paragraph cover letter that emphasizes my customer-facing experience and genuine interest in sustainability."*

### Specify format and length

*"Explain compound interest in under 150 words, using a simple example with real numbers."*

*"Give me a bulleted list of 8 questions I should ask before signing a lease."*

### Ask it to show its reasoning

For complex or factual questions, ask: *"Walk me through your reasoning step by step"* or *"What are you less certain about in this answer?"*

This surfaces uncertainty that would otherwise stay hidden.

### Iterate

Treat the first response as a draft. Common follow-up prompts:
- *"Make this shorter and more direct"*
- *"The second paragraph is too technical — simplify it"*
- *"This sounds too formal — make it conversational"*
- *"What's a counterargument to this?"*

## The Verification Habit

For anything factual — especially anything you'll repeat, publish, or act on — verify it from a primary source.

A good workflow:
1. Ask the AI to explain or summarize something
2. Note any specific claims, statistics, or citations it makes
3. Search for those specific facts from original sources (news articles, official documents, peer-reviewed papers)
4. If you can't verify it, treat it as uncertain

This sounds like extra work, but it only takes a few minutes for most things, and it prevents embarrassing (or costly) mistakes.

## Practical Use Cases for Financial Learning

AI tools are especially useful for financial education, with some caveats:

**Good uses:**
- Explaining what a Roth IRA is and how it works
- Walking through how to read a pay stub
- Describing what "vesting" means in a 401(k) context
- Comparing the general pros and cons of different budgeting methods
- Explaining what "expense ratio" means and why it matters

**Proceed with caution:**
- Asking for specific investment recommendations (the AI doesn't know your situation, risk tolerance, or tax circumstances)
- Asking for specific tax advice (always consult a CPA or enrolled agent)
- Assuming AI-cited statistics are accurate without checking

The sweet spot is using AI to build your conceptual understanding, then verifying specifics and getting personalized advice from qualified humans.
