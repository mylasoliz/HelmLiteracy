Warren Buffett — arguably the greatest investor of all time — has publicly stated that when he dies, he wants 90% of the money left to his wife invested in a simple S&P 500 index fund. Not in a hedge fund. Not managed by a professional stockpicker. A simple, boring index fund.

Why? Because the data is overwhelming: most actively managed funds underperform index funds over the long run, especially after fees.

## What Is an Index Fund?

An index is a list of securities (usually stocks) that represents some slice of the market. The S&P 500, for example, is a list of the 500 largest publicly traded companies in the United States.

An **index fund** is an investment that simply buys all the stocks in an index, in the same proportions, and holds them. It doesn't try to pick winners. It doesn't have a manager making active decisions. It just mirrors the index.

Because there's no active management, fees are extremely low — often 0.03% to 0.10% per year compared to 1–2% for actively managed funds.

## Why Do Index Funds Outperform?

Three reasons, compounding over time:

### 1. Fees matter more than you think

If you invest $10,000 and earn 7% per year:

- At a **0.04% fee** (typical index fund): you end up with **$76,122** after 30 years
- At a **1.00% fee** (typical active fund): you end up with **$57,435** after 30 years

That 0.96% difference costs you nearly **$20,000** — roughly 20% of your final balance.

### 2. Markets are hard to beat consistently

Professional fund managers have access to analysts, research, and sophisticated tools. Yet study after study shows that over a 15-year period, roughly **85–90% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index**. The few that do beat it rarely repeat that performance.

### 3. Diversification is built in

When you buy an S&P 500 index fund, you own a tiny piece of 500 companies across every sector. If one company goes bankrupt, it barely affects your investment. Concentration risk — the risk of having too much in one stock — is essentially eliminated.

## Types of Index Funds

**Mutual funds vs. ETFs** — Both can track an index, but they work differently:

- **Index mutual funds** are priced once per day after markets close. You buy or sell at the end-of-day price. Minimum investments often apply.
- **ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)** trade throughout the day like stocks. Most have no minimum investment and can be bought for the price of one share (often $1–$100 with fractional shares).

For most beginners, either works fine. ETFs are slightly more flexible.

## Which Index Should You Track?

| Fund | What It Tracks | Example Ticker |
|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 | 500 largest US companies | VOO, SPY, FXAIX |
| Total US Market | All US public companies (~4,000) | VTI, FSKAX |
| Total World | US + international stocks | VT |
| US Bonds | US government/corporate bonds | BND |

For a beginner, a **Total US Market** or **S&P 500** fund covers most of what you need. Adding an international fund gives broader diversification.

## How to Start

1. **Open a brokerage account.** Fidelity, Vanguard, and Schwab are the most popular for individual investors. If you're investing for retirement, open a Roth IRA first — your gains grow tax-free.

2. **Search for a low-cost index fund.** Look for an expense ratio under 0.10%. On Fidelity, for example, FZROX has a 0% expense ratio.

3. **Set up automatic contributions.** Decide on an amount you can invest every month — even $50 is a start. Automate it so you invest regardless of what the market is doing.

4. **Don't touch it.** This is the hardest part. Markets will drop. Headlines will be scary. The strategy only works if you stay invested through downturns.

## The Simplest Possible Portfolio

For someone just starting out:

- **100% VTI** (Total US Market ETF) — simple, diversified, low-cost

Or if you want international exposure:
- **80% VTI + 20% VXUS** (International stocks)

That's it. Two funds, automatic contributions, and time. The data shows this approach beats most professional investors over a 20-year horizon.

The goal isn't to find the hottest stock — it's to capture the market's growth over time. Index funds do that better than almost anything else, at the lowest possible cost.
