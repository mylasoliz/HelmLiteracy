Your credit score is one of the most consequential numbers in your financial life. It affects the interest rate you pay on car loans and mortgages, whether you can rent an apartment, and in some states, even whether you get certain jobs.

Yet most people have only a vague idea of how it's calculated. Let's fix that.

## The FICO Score: The One That Actually Matters

There are many credit scoring models, but the one lenders use most is the **FICO Score**, which ranges from 300 to 850. Here's how the ranges are generally interpreted:

| Score Range | Rating |
|---|---|
| 800–850 | Exceptional |
| 740–799 | Very Good |
| 670–739 | Good |
| 580–669 | Fair |
| Below 580 | Poor |

A score above 740 generally gets you the best interest rates. Below 620, and many lenders will decline you or charge significantly higher rates.

## The Five Factors — and Their Real Weight

FICO breaks down its calculation into five categories:

### 1. Payment History — 35%

This is the biggest factor by far. It answers one question: do you pay your bills on time?

A single late payment (30+ days past due) can drop your score by 60–110 points, depending on how high it was to begin with. The impact fades over time but remains on your report for 7 years.

**What to do:** Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment on every account. Never, ever miss a payment intentionally.

### 2. Credit Utilization — 30%

This is the percentage of your available credit that you're using. If you have a $10,000 credit limit and carry a $3,000 balance, your utilization is 30%.

The lower, the better. Below 30% is the commonly cited threshold, but the highest scorers typically stay below 10%.

**Counterintuitive fact:** Utilization is recalculated monthly based on your statement balance, not your average balance. Paying your full balance before the statement closing date — not just the due date — can dramatically lower your reported utilization.

**What to do:** Pay balances down before statements close. If you need to make large purchases, ask your card issuer for a credit limit increase (which lowers your utilization ratio without changing your balance).

### 3. Length of Credit History — 15%

FICO looks at the age of your oldest account, your newest account, and the average age of all accounts. Older is better.

**What to do:** Don't close old credit cards even if you rarely use them. Closing an old card lowers your average account age and reduces your available credit (raising your utilization). Put a small recurring charge on old cards to keep them active.

### 4. Credit Mix — 10%

Lenders like to see that you can manage different types of credit: credit cards (revolving), auto loans, mortgages, student loans (installment). Having a mix shows broader creditworthiness.

**What to do:** Don't open accounts just for the mix. But having both a credit card and an installment loan (like a student or auto loan) is slightly better than only one type.

### 5. New Credit — 10%

Every time you apply for new credit, the lender performs a "hard inquiry" that temporarily dings your score by 5–10 points. Multiple inquiries in a short period signal financial stress to lenders.

**Exception:** Multiple mortgage or auto loan inquiries within a 14–45 day window are typically counted as a single inquiry, since it's normal to rate-shop for these.

**What to do:** Only apply for credit when you actually need it. Avoid opening multiple new accounts in a short period.

## Building Credit from Scratch

If you have little or no credit history, you can't get a credit card because you have no credit — and you can't build credit without a card. Here's how to break the cycle:

**Secured credit card.** You deposit money (say, $200–$500) as collateral and get a card with that as your limit. Use it lightly, pay it off monthly, and after 6–12 months you typically qualify for a regular card. Many issuers (Discover, Capital One) will upgrade you automatically.

**Credit-builder loan.** Offered by credit unions and online lenders. You make monthly payments into a savings account; at the end of the loan term, you receive the money. The payment history gets reported to credit bureaus.

**Become an authorized user.** If a parent or close friend with excellent credit adds you as an authorized user on their card, their history on that card appears on your credit report. You don't even need to use the card.

## Checking Your Score (for Free)

You're entitled to one free credit report per year from each of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) at **AnnualCreditReport.com** — the only federally mandated source. Check all three, since errors on one bureau don't automatically appear on others.

For ongoing score monitoring, most credit cards now provide your FICO score for free on your monthly statement or app. Credit Karma and Credit Sesame show scores for free but use VantageScore, which is slightly different from FICO.

---

Credit scores reward consistent, boring behavior over time: pay on time, keep balances low, don't open too many new accounts. There are no shortcuts — but the good news is that even a poor score can recover substantially within 12–24 months of disciplined habits.
